Strategy / Concept 02

Conceptual Framework

Inversion

"Invert, always invert: Turn a situation or problem upside down. Look at it backward." — Carl Jacobi

The Core Thesis

Inversion is one of the most powerful mental models because it forces you to approach problems from the opposite end of the natural starting point. While most people focus on what they want to happen (the positive outcome), inversion focuses on what you want to avoid (the negative outcome).

By thinking about what would cause a project to fail, or what would make you unhappy, you can create a "pre-mortem" that identifies risks before they manifest. It is often easier to identify obstacles to success than it is to identify the precise path to achievement.

How to Apply

Forward Thinking

"How can I make this product a massive success and gain 1 million users?"

Inverse Thinking

"What would definitely make users hate this product and delete it within minutes?"

Practical Examples

  • / Investing: Instead of looking for the "next big winner," Charlie Munger suggested looking for ways to lose money and then avoiding them.
  • / Productivity: Instead of "How can I be more productive?" ask "What are the three things that destroy my focus most?" and eliminate them.
  • / Relationships: Ask "What behaviors would destroy my connection with my partner?" then make it a rule never to engage in them.

Key Principles

  • Avoidance Over Optimization
    It's often more effective to remove the "stupid" than to seek the "brilliant."
  • The Pre-Mortem
    Imagine the project has failed six months from now. Write down why.
  • Subtract, Don't Add
    Inversion naturally leads to a via negativa approach to problem solving.
