Ockham's Razor

"Plurality should not be posited without necessity."

Ockham's Razor is a problem-solving principle that suggests when presented with competing hypotheses about the same prediction, one should select the solution with the fewest assumptions. It is not a proof of truth, but a preference for simplicity.

In analytical work, the 'razor' serves to shave off unnecessary complexity that often obscures the most likely path forward. The more variables you add to a model, the more points of failure you introduce.

Complexity vs. Utility

Overfitting

Creating elaborate explanations to fit every data point perfectly. High internal validity, but poor predictive power in real-world scenarios.

Parsimony

The quality of being frugal with assumptions. Robust, adaptable models that focus on the signal rather than the noise.

While often misinterpreted as "the simplest answer is usually right," the principle is more precisely about the economy of thought. We should only add complexity when the evidence demands it.

  • List all assumptions required for each competing explanation.
  • Prioritize the explanation that relies on the fewest unproven links.
  • Maintain the simpler model until evidence necessitates a more complex one.

By adhering to parsimony, we build systems that are easier to maintain, explain, and evolve over time.

Key Principles

Parsimony
The scientific principle that things are usually connected or behave in the simplest way.
Eliminative Induction
Systematically removing improbable explanations to narrow the field of inquiry.
Falsifiability
Ensuring that a hypothesis can be proven wrong by observation or experiment.

Curator's Note

The razor is a tool for navigation, not a destination. It helps us find the most efficient starting point, but we must never ignore data simply because it makes our model more complex.
