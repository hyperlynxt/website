The Map is Not the Territory

Long-form Definition

The "map is not the territory" is a conceptual model that asserts that an abstraction derived from something, or a reaction to it, is not the thing itself. An individual's perception of the world is not the world itself, but rather an internal representation—a "map"—of that world.

The model emphasizes that descriptions of reality are not reality. Because of the human brain's limited bandwidth and cognitive constraints, we must simplify complex realities into manageable mental frameworks. While these maps are essential for navigation and decision-making, mistaking them for reality leads to critical errors in judgment and systemic failure.

Historical Context & Origin

"A map is not the territory it represents, but, if correct, it has a similar structure to the territory, which accounts for its usefulness."
— Alfred Korzybski, 1931

The phrase was coined by Polish-American philosopher Alfred Korzybski at a meeting of the American Association for the Advancement of Science in New Orleans, Louisiana. It was a foundational principle of General Semantics, a discipline Korzybski developed to improve how humans relate to the world and to each other through the study of language and symbolism.
