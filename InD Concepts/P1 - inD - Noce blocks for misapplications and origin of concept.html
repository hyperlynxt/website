Inversion

The Art of Thinking Backward

Via Negativa Margin of Safety Falsification
Popularised By Charlie Munger & Carl Jacobi
Origin 19th-century German mathematics
Difficulty
Moderate
Reading Time 6 min

"Invert, always invert: Turn a situation or problem upside down. Look at it backward. What happens if all our plans go wrong?"

Inversion is the practice of thinking about problems in reverse. Instead of asking how to achieve a desired outcome, you ask how to achieve the opposite — and then avoid doing those things.

Common Misapplications

The Flaw: Passive Pessimism

Using inversion as an excuse to simply list everything that can go wrong without taking action to mitigate them.

Correction: Active Elimination

Treat the failure list as a checklist of specific behaviors to be ruthlessly engineered out of the process.

The Flaw: Only Inverting

Relying solely on backward thinking while ignoring the need for a positive forward strategy or vision.

Correction: Dual Process

Use inversion to define the "floor" of safety, but maintain forward thinking to define the "ceiling" of success.

The Flaw: Constant Reversal

Applying inversion to trivial or low-stakes decisions where the cost of thinking exceeds the benefit of avoidance.

Correction: Strategic Sizing

Reserve inversion for complex systems and high-stakes outcomes where ruin is a non-zero possibility.

The Dual Process

Forward Thinking

Focuses on the optimal path. Requires precise execution and the assumption that all positive variables will align.

Backward Thinking

Focuses on structural resilience. Identifies catastrophic failure points and actively engineers them out.
