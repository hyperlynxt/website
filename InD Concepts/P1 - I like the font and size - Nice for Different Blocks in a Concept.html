Strategy / Concept 02
Observation Models

Take notice of what light does.

To everything, light brings structure, shadow, and truth. Without changing the object itself, it completely alters how the object is perceived.

The Phenomenon

In physical space, lighting determines hierarchy. A harsh spotlight demands immediate attention, obliterating subtle details in its glare, while washing out the periphery into deep shadow. Conversely, diffuse ambient light reveals texture and nuance across a broad surface evenly, treating all areas with democratic visibility.

When we apply this as a mental model to information architecture and systems design, "light" translates to cognitive focus and interface contrast.

Diffuse State

Ambient Awareness

Information exists in harmony. Used for exploration, reading, and deep comprehension where context matters as much as the subject.

Focused State

Directed Attention

High contrast forces a singular path. Used for critical actions, alerts, and isolated tasks where distraction is a liability.

Application in Design

  • Determine the light source: What is the primary objective of the screen? Let that be the brightest point.
  • Embrace the shadows: Not everything needs high contrast. Muted tones and lower opacities create necessary depth and reduce cognitive load.
  • Avoid multiple suns: An interface with too many high-contrast, competing elements creates visual anxiety and destroys hierarchy.
  • Observe edge cases: Just as light behaves differently at the edge of an object, pay attention to how systems degrade or transition at their boundaries.

Understanding this model allows a designer to choreograph user attention rather than simply laying out elements on a grid.

Key Principles
Focus vs. Context
Light dictates whether we see the detail of the leaf or the shape of the tree.
Negative Space as Shadow
Shadow is not empty; it is the space that gives the light definition.
Directional Cues
The angle of illumination guides the eye naturally across a surface.
