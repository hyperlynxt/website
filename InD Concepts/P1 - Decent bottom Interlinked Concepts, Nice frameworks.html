Inversion

Decision Making
Core Model · 5 Min Read · Charlie Munger

"Approaching a situation from the opposite end of the natural starting point. Instead of asking how to achieve success, ask how to guarantee failure, and avoid those actions."

Inversion is a foundational mental model that asks us to flip a problem backward to reveal its inherent vulnerabilities. The root of this concept is often attributed to the 19th-century German mathematician Carl Gustav Jacob Jacobi, who famously instructed his students, "man muss immer umkehren" (invert, always invert). Jacobi believed that the solution to many complex mathematical problems became obvious only when expressed in inverse form.

While born in mathematics, inversion was popularized in modern strategic thinking by Charlie Munger in the context of investing and life choices. Munger noted that many hard problems are best solved when they are addressed backward. By studying what creates the worst possible outcomes, we create a robust roadmap for what to avoid, which is often a more reliable path to success than trying to orchestrate brilliance.

Mechanics & Application

The human brain is naturally wired for forward-thinking—we set goals and plot paths toward them. However, our ability to predict complex future outcomes is notoriously poor. We are often much better at identifying what is wrong, dangerous, or stupid than we are at identifying what is perfectly right.

"It is remarkable how much long-term advantage people like us have gotten by trying to be consistently not stupid, instead of trying to be very intelligent."
— Charlie Munger

By forcing yourself to look at the inverse of a desired outcome, you illuminate hidden risks and cognitive blind spots that a forward-thinking approach might easily gloss over. It shifts the perspective from seeking the elusive 'best' path to systematically eliminating the worst ones. Success becomes a process of subtraction rather than addition.

Practical Usages

Engineering Failure Analysis

Instead of designing a bridge with the sole intention of keeping it up, structural engineers design assuming it will fall down, meticulously calculating all the environmental forces and stress limits that could cause failure, and then building margins of safety against those exact vectors.

Investment Thesis Stress-Testing

Rather than looking for confirming evidence of why a stock will go up (which leads to confirmation bias), a rigorous investor searches for every possible reason the business could fail, the market could shift, or their thesis could be entirely wrong.

Argument Steelmanning

To truly understand a debate or validate a philosophical position, one must be able to argue the opposing side better than the opposition themselves. Inverting your perspective exposes the weakest links in your own foundational logic.
